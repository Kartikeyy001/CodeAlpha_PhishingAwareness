What is Phishing?

Phishing is a social engineering attack that uses fraudulent emails, websites, or messages to trick people into:

  • Revealing sensitive information (passwords, SSN, financial data)
  • Downloading malware or ransomware
  • Transferring money to fraudulent accounts
  • Granting unauthorized access to systems

Case Study: Sony Pictures Hack (2014)

How it happened: Spear phishing emails impersonating Apple, targeting Sony employees. When clicked, malware gave attackers full access.

Impact: Private emails leaked, actor salaries exposed, unreleased movies stolen, $100M+ loss

Why it worked: Familiar brand (Apple) + routine request + no employee training

How Attackers Hunt You: The 4 Phases

Phase 1: RECONNAISSANCE (OSINT)

LinkedIn profiles, company websites, job postings, social media - attackers build your digital profile without you knowing

Phase 2: ENGAGEMENT (Build Trust)

Personalized message using your real name, job role, projects - looks like it's from someone who knows you

Phase 3: MANIPULATION (Psychological Trigger)

Urgency, authority, fear, greed - attackers exploit how your brain makes decisions under pressure

Phase 4: ACTION (You Comply)

Click link, enter password, download file, wire money - attack succeeds in seconds

Scenario 1: The LinkedIn Job Offer Trap

LinkedIn message: "Congratulations! We want to hire you as Senior Security Engineer. Click here to complete background check." The link is a bit_ly shortened URL from someone claiming to be from Google.

Attack Type: Spear phishing using LinkedIn (OSINT) + shortened URLs to hide malicious destination

Scenario 2: The CEO Wire Transfer Trick

Email from CEO (name correct, signature matches): "Need you to wire $50K to this new vendor TODAY. Keep this between us. Time-sensitive." Email address looks slightly off but similar (ceo@company.co instead of ceo@company.com).

Attack Type: Business Email Compromise (BEC) - $46.7M stolen from Ubiquiti Networks using exact same tactic

Scenario 3: The USB Drive Gamble

You find a USB drive in the company parking lot labeled "Q4 Budget Review - CONFIDENTIAL" with your company logo. Inside could be important documents... or malware. What happens if you plug it in?

Attack Type: Baiting with physical media - one USB drive = ransomware across entire network

If You Suspect You've Been Phished

  1. Stop immediately: Don't click more links or download anything
  2. Report to IT/Security: Alert your organization's security team right away
  3. Change passwords: Update passwords for affected accounts from a safe device
  4. Monitor accounts: Watch for unauthorized transactions or login attempts
  5. Enable MFA: Add multi-factor authentication to critical accounts
  6. Don't panic: Early reporting can prevent significant damage
